What is Voltage, Current, and Resistance?

Electricity in a circuit involves charge-carrying electrons moving along a wire, and can be understood the same way as water flowing through a hose. Voltage is similar to water pressure, Current is similar to the flow of water, and resistance is the thickness of the hose. To help understand this, imagine a gardener with a hose and no water flowing through it. As he turns the faucet the water pressure increases, and as the pressure increases, the water flows faster. Also note that the thickness of the hose remains the same throughout. Now imagine the gardener watering a garden, with the hose on full blast. The pressure of the water is high, and the water is flowing very fast. Now by accident, the gardener steps on the hose, making it half as thick at one point. Naturally the water flows much slower, but the water pressure from the faucet is still full blast. A circuit works just like this scenario. If the Resistance of a circuit (or thickness of a hose) remains unchanged as the Voltage (water pressure) is increased, the Current (flow) will increase. But if the Resistance decreases but Voltage remains the same, the Current will decrease. This relationship between Voltage, Resistance and Current is described by Ohm’s Law: (Voltage) = (Current )* (Resistance), or V=IR. All circuits are described with these properties.

He accomplishes this by attaching a Y shaped nozzle to the hose and attaching a hose to each end. To his dismay, the total water flow in each hose is half the total flow, even though the original faucet is full blast. More water would flow through the thicker hose since it is easier to flow into. A thinner hose is analogous to a greater resistance since a thinner hose makes water harder to flow through, just as it is harder for electrons to flow through a higher resistance. One important principle with resistive circuits is that electrons will always flow through the path of least resistance.
